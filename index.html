The Problem — Starting Idea

I joined Silicon Jackets because I was genuinely curious about chip design. Coming in as a Computer Engineering student, I knew hardware existed — but I had a vague, surface-level understanding of what designing a chip actually involved. I assumed it was mostly physical wiring and circuit diagrams. The reality turned out to be far more interesting, and far more connected to the software world I was already exploring.

My goal going in was simple: complete the onboarding and figure out whether hardware was a direction I wanted to pursue seriously. What I didn't expect was that the answer would lead me somewhere much more specific.

The Process — Three Tracks, Three Perspectives

Silicon Jackets onboarding runs across three parallel tracks, each covering a different phase of the chip design flow. I completed all three.

Digital Design

RTL Design

Designed a 64-bit calculator system in SystemVerilog — including a 32-bit adder, FSM controller, result buffer, and full top-level integration with an SRAM macro. Learned combinational vs. sequential logic, memory-mapped interfaces, and waveform-based debugging in Cadence Xcelium.

Design Verification

UVM Testbench

Built a full parameterized UVM testbench — driver, monitor, scoreboard, sequencer — for the 32/64-bit SRAM calculator, achieving 99.7% functional coverage and 100% assertion pass rate. DV ended up being the track that clicked most naturally for me.

Physical Design

RTL to GDS

Took the design through the full physical implementation flow in Cadence Innovus — from synthesis to place-and-route to signoff. Resolved timing violations (WNS from -2.701ns to +0.01ns) and eliminated 1,000 DRC violations to achieve a clean GDS. This was the hardest track by far.

Successes and Failures

The biggest challenge was the sheer amount of new material across all three tracks simultaneously. Every tool, every concept, and every workflow was brand new — there was no prior experience to lean on. Physical Design in particular was a steep climb. The EDA tools are complex, the failure modes are cryptic, and the gap between "it simulates" and "it's physically realizable" is larger than it looks from the outside.

Design Verification was the opposite — it clicked almost immediately. Writing the testbench felt closer to software engineering than traditional hardware design, and I found I had a natural instinct for it. Hitting 99.7% functional coverage on the first major project was a genuine milestone.

The biggest success was simply finishing all three tracks. Most members specialize in one. Completing the full flow gave me a picture of how hardware actually gets built from specification to silicon — a perspective most software engineers never develop.

ECE Skills Gained

Coming in, I could write Python and build web applications. Coming out, I can design and verify digital hardware at a professional level. Specific skills developed:

SystemVerilog RTL Design FSM Design and Control UVM Verification Methodology Functional Coverage Analysis Cadence Xcelium / Innovus Timing Closure and SDC Constraints DRC Violation Resolution Full RTL-to-GDS Flow Make-based EDA Automation

Outcome — What This Changed

Completing the Silicon Jackets onboarding didn't just teach me hardware — it clarified my entire direction. Working on the processor simulator made something click: making AI faster is not an AI problem, it is a systems problem. Performance is constrained less by model design than by how efficiently data moves through memory and hardware. That realization, combined with my software work on Degree Roadmap and HexLabs, pointed me toward AI infrastructure engineering as a precise career target. I've since joined the Silicon Jackets simulator team, where I'm building a cycle-accurate RISC-V superscalar CPU simulator in C++ — the natural continuation of everything this project started.
